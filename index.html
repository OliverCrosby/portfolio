ArriveCAN: Fighting for Users Inside a Bureaucratic Nightmare

The Real Story

Let me be honest about ArriveCAN. You probably remember it as that app everyone hated during COVID. The one that became a political punching bag. It had serious problems. But MY role in the project tells a story of success, so I'm going to tell it anyway! Here's what actually happened behind the scenes: I joined a project where the government contracted devs who didn't even know what the Web Experience Toolkit was (that's like a web developer not knowing HTML). Despite fighting through 50+ layers of bureaucracy just to get a single change request to the dev team, I managed to drive adoption from 13% to 80% before they made the app mandatory. This is how I did it.

The Challenge (The Official Version vs Reality)

Official version: "Increase digital adoption to streamline border processing."

Reality: I was brought in for "quality assurance" but quickly discovered the app had fundamental UX problems that QA alone couldn't fix. Although we (my team at PHAC) didn't know it at the time, the development had been outsourced to incompetent contractors who had to Google basic government web standards apparently had very little experience developing apps. Every fix required approval from dozens of bureaucrats who'd never used the app themselves. So my primary challenge was finding minimal changes with maximum impact and then fighting to get them through a bureaucratic machine to the fabled 'dev' team that I never actually met.

What I Actually Did (And Got Done)

Despite the dysfunction, I carved out space to do real UX research. I surveyed 1,719 travelers, conducted usability testing, and built data visualizations that even bureaucrats couldn't ignore. My role officially stayed "policy analyst" (government job titles are vague like that), but I became the de facto UX lead because nobody else was asking basic questions like "can users actually complete this form?"

Key Insights From My Research

Check out the Tableau dashboard I built to see the full analysis. The data told a clear story:

The shocking finding: 44% of travelers had never even heard of ArriveCAN. Not "chose not to use it"—literally didn't know it existed. Meanwhile, another 31% knew about it but chose paper because the app seemed harder. This wasn't a technology problem; it was a massive communication and design failure.

The Critical Issues I Found (That Never Got Fixed)

Account creation disaster: 11 out of 20 test users couldn't find the "Create Account" link. It was buried below the login button. Basic UX 101 failure.

WCAG violations everywhere: Full-width text on desktop (unreadable for low vision users), missing radio buttons for screen readers, navigation that would accidentally sign users out mid-form.

What I Actually Achieved

Despite all the bureaucratic roadblocks, I did drive real change:

13% → 80%
App adoption before the app was mandatory (yes, really)
1,719
Users surveyed for actionable insights
23 → 12
Steps reduced in user flow (when they listened)
7
Languages supported after my push

The adoption increase happened because I bypassed the bureaucracy where I could. I worked directly with airlines to get ArriveCAN mentioned in booking emails. I got QR codes added to airport signage. I pushed for simplified forms that actually worked on phones. Every small win required enormous effort, but they added up.

The Bitter Truth

Once the government made the app mandatory, they basically stopped listening to user feedback. My carefully documented accessibility violations? Ignored. The critical usability issues? "Not a priority." The bureaucrats got their compliance numbers and stopped caring about user experience. The contractors kept getting paid despite their incompetence. And ArriveCAN became the controvercial app you remember (if you ever used it, or read about it in the news).

But here's what I learned: even in the most dysfunctional environment, good UX research can drive real change. I couldn't fix everything, but I made life easier for millions of travelers during a global pandemic. And I have the data to prove it.

Why This Matters for AI Tool Design

ArriveCAN taught me how to fight for users when the organization doesn't want to listen. That 44% who never heard of the app? That's exactly like mainstream users who don't know AI tools could help them. The 31% who thought paper was easier? That's your AI-skeptical users who need convincing that new technology is worth their time.

Most importantly, this project taught me that real user adoption comes from obsessive attention to actual user needs, not what stakeholders think users need. When Mozilla says they want to bring AI to the masses while respecting privacy and user autonomy, I know exactly what that fight looks like. I've been in those meetings where everyone wants to take shortcuts on accessibility. I've pushed back when "good enough" meant excluding vulnerable users. And I've proven I can drive adoption even when half the organization is working against me.

Note: All data and findings presented here are from public government sources or anonymized research I conducted. No confidential information has been disclosed. The views expressed are my own professional observations.

ComfyUI Minimap: Simplifying AI Workflow Navigation

The Problem

ComfyUI is a powerful node-based interface for Stable Diffusion workflows, but as workflows grow increasingly complex, users struggle with spatial navigation. Working on elaborate image generation pipelines, I found myself constantly lost in sprawling node graphs -a problem shared by the entire ComfyUI community.

The Solution

Drawing inspiration from RTS game design, I created a minimalist minimap overlay that provides instant spatial context without disrupting the workflow. The design prioritizes clarity and performance, rendering hundreds of nodes in real-time while maintaining a lightweight footprint.

Key Design Decisions

  • • Minimal visual hierarchy: Simplified node representation to reduce cognitive load
  • • Smart positioning: Non-intrusive corner placement with collision detection
  • • Progressive disclosure: Hide/show toggle for uninterrupted focus work
  • • Performance-first: Optimized rendering for complex workflows with 500+ nodes

Image Sorter: Streamlining High-Volume Manual Tasks

The Problem

Manually sorting through thousands of images is tedious and time-consuming, requiring repetitive decisions and actions that quickly lead to fatigue and errors.

The Solution

I built a Flask-based single-interface tool that allows users to add custom sorting options and modifications, dramatically speeding up the image organization process. The interface focuses on keyboard-driven interactions and customizable workflows to minimize friction.

Key Design Decisions

  • • Single interface focus: Uninterrupted workflow design prevents context switching
  • • Keyboard-first interaction: Hotkeys for rapid image processing and sorting
  • • Customizable categories: User-defined sorting options adapt to specific workflows
  • • Lightweight architecture: Flask-based local operation with minimal resource usage

Data Strategy Website: Making Strategy Documents People Actually Use

Project Overview

Transformed the Public Health Agency's first Data Strategy from a traditional 50-page PDF into an interactive, exploratory website. Government strategy documents typically achieve less than 10% engagement. Our interactive website reached 85% content engagement by reimagining how staff interact with strategic guidance.

The Challenge

Staff needed strategic guidance but lacked time to read lengthy PDFs. Traditional strategy documents were linear, text-heavy, and disconnected from daily workflows. The agency needed a way to make strategic information discoverable, actionable, and relevant to diverse roles across 15 departments.

My Role & Approach

I led the end-to-end design and development of the interactive strategy website. After facilitating organization-wide consultations and conducting the initial research that shaped the strategy content, I analyzed 47 similar government documents, interviewed staff across departments to understand consumption patterns, and designed a question-based information architecture that crossed traditional document boundaries.

Key Insights

Information Architecture Innovation

Instead of forcing users through linear document structure, I organized content around the questions they actually ask:

  • • "What does this mean for my role?"
  • • "What do I need to do this quarter?"
  • • "How does this connect to other initiatives?"
  • • "What resources are available?"

Progressive Disclosure Design

Created modular content blocks with multiple access paths through a sophisticated tagging system. Users could explore strategy elements based on their role, department, timeline, or specific project needs—finding relevant information 3x faster than traditional navigation.

Design Solutions

Developed a "choose your own adventure" approach to complex policy areas using GitHub Pages within government infrastructure constraints. Built reusable template system adopted by other departments. Implemented visual flows connecting high-level goals to specific tasks, making abstract strategy concrete and actionable.

Why This Matters for AI Tool Design

This project demonstrates my ability to transform complex, abstract concepts into intuitive, actionable interfaces—exactly what's needed for AI features in Firefox. Most importantly, achieving 85% engagement with traditionally ignored content proves I can drive adoption of new technologies through thoughtful information architecture and user-centered design.
